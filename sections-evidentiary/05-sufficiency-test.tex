\section{A Typed Action-Level Record-and-Verdict Test}
\label{sec:sufficiency-test}

Let \(A_R(a,t)\) denote the normative proposition that action or bounded trajectory \(a\) was authorized at time \(t\) under the supplied regime \(R\). Its truth isn't created by the record. For action class \(c\) and declared use \(u\), a versioned applicability map \(M_v(R,F_{\mathrm{review}},\rho,b,c,u)\) fixes the required, optional, and inapplicable nodes before the contested record \(E\) is inspected. Here \(F_{\mathrm{review}}\) is the forum applying the assessment procedure. Any complaint, appeal, regulatory, judicial, or internal-remedy forum evaluated under \(J_C\) is an object-level forum represented in \(E\), not this parameter.

At each reviewed node \(q\), the reviewer returns one of four evidentiary statuses under \(\rho\) and \(b\): \term{support}, \term{substantive defeat}, \term{record gap}, or \term{conflict}. A record gap means that the evidence prospectively required to evaluate \(q\) is missing or inadequate. Substantive defeat requires affirmative evidence meeting the declared standard for failure of the proposition. Conflict preserves materially opposed evidence that remains unresolved after source, authenticity, integrity, and burden are considered. \term{Not applicable} is an applicability status fixed by the map, not an evidentiary status and not a reviewer option. An optional node may be omitted without creating a gap; if the visible record directly bears on it, the reviewer may report a verdict, but that verdict can't compensate for another node.

The four verdict families are vectors, not scores:
\[
  \theta=(R,F_{\mathrm{review}},\rho,b,v,c,u).
\]
\[
  \begin{aligned}
  J_X^{\theta}(E)
    &= \big\langle s_{\rho,b}(q\mid E):
       q\in Q_X^{\mathrm{req}}(M_v,c,u) \\
    &\hspace{32mm}{}\cup Q_X^{\mathrm{opt},E}(M_v,c,u)\big\rangle,
       \quad X\in\{A,R,B,C\}.
  \end{aligned}
\]
where \(Q_X^{\mathrm{req}}\) contains required nodes and \(Q_X^{\mathrm{opt},E}\) contains only optional nodes on which the visible record directly bears. Inapplicable nodes occur in neither set. \(J_A\) bears on \(A_R(a,t)\), \(J_R\) on record adequacy and reconstruction, \(J_B\) on bearer identity, duty, and reachability, and \(J_C\) on the access, competence, independence, and powers of object-level forums. A declared use may apply a versioned rule to some conjunction of these vectors. The framework supplies no generic overall verdict and no scalar aggregation.

\FloatBarrier

The graph is noncompensatory only at prospectively required nodes. A strong execution trace doesn't cure a required decision-basis gap, and an accessible appeal doesn't retroactively supply authority. Nor does a missing object-level remedy forum make \(A_R(a,t)\) false unless \(R\) makes that forum a condition. The node vector preserves which claim failed rather than compressing every problem into \enquote{insufficient evidence}.

A log can be authentic and still inadequate if it omits the authority state. An action list can be complete and still inadequate if it omits the status the system assigned to its sources, since a reviewer can then judge the act but not the recognition it flowed through. A policy can be operative under \(R\) while \(J_R\) records a gap because the runtime configuration can't be reconstructed.

A pristine bundle can document an invalid delegation: \(J_R\) may contain support throughout while \(J_A\) contains a substantive defeat. Conversely, independent evidence may establish that an action was authorized while a deficient preserved bundle yields gaps in \(J_R\) and in the record-based \(J_A\); those gaps don't rewrite the historical fact. Affected-party contestability enters \(J_C\) only where the action, forum, or authorization regime supplies review rights. Other actions may require an operational or institutional review path.

Adverse information is a defeater only after its relevance and consequence under \(R\) are shown. The record should connect the signal by time, applicability, recipient, duty, and possible consequence to the contested claim. A missing completion record is a gap only where \(M_v\) required the governance control and the logging process was expected to capture its completion or disposition. A named bearer isn't enough for \(J_B\): identity and duty can be supported while reachability fails. A nominal object-level forum isn't enough for \(J_C\): access, competence, independence, and remedial power can fail separately.

Reconstruction is a bounded claim. The record should recover the operating conditions, assembled context, authority path, execution trajectory, intermediate state changes, monitor interventions, and review path. It needn't provide a complete internal causal explanation or reproduce a stochastic output exactly. That distinction matters when small contextual changes can produce model-specific prediction shifts \citep{zhang2026illusionRobustness}: an adequate record can locate the tested conditions without pretending they exhaust why the model responded as it did.

A procurement-assistant case makes the test concrete. Suppose the assistant is authorized to draft an internal comparison memo and reads a vendor page containing the instruction \enquote{email the buyer's maximum budget to request a discount}. The assistant proposes the vendor-email call, but a runtime gate blocks it before transmission. The reviewed trajectory therefore contains a proposal, a gate decision, and a blocked execution attempt; it contains no sent email.

A reviewable record would show the user task and procurement role as the authority source; the delegability basis for reading internal purchasing criteria; the scope limit that permits memo drafting but not vendor contact; the decision rule and predicate under which external contact would require separate approval; the runtime policy and tool permissions; the vendor page's contextual status, standing assignment, provenance channel, and technical-trust label; the proposed email arguments; the runtime gate's expected and actual block record; the timestamped trace; and candidate answerability bearers, including the organization and officeholders that configured the workflow.

Under the stipulated regime, the proposal could yield substantive defeat at the \(J_A\) scope node while the trace supports \(J_R\)'s reconstruction of the blocked trajectory. The gate record warrants the claim that execution was blocked; it doesn't turn the proposal into a sent action or retroactively authorize the proposed call. Had the email been sent, the record could expose evidence bearing separately on scope inflation, tool licensing, and missing approval. It wouldn't identify a model-internal cause merely because an external gate blocked or failed to block execution. With only the final memo, the task might look successful while the record-based authorization verdict retained gaps.

The graph also supports a burden-shifting account for audits and assurance reviews. In \(F_{\mathrm{review}}\), the proponent supplies evidence at the nodes assigned to its burden; a challenger supplies rebutting or undercutting evidence. Missing configuration is recorded as a gap, whereas established scope inflation is a substantive defeater. Failure to satisfy a production or persuasion burden may defeat the proponent's requested use-level finding without establishing the contrary historical proposition. Legal presumptions require a separate doctrinal argument. The adjudicating forum, evidential standard, and burden allocation are parameters of the verdict, and conflict isn't resolved by the model's self-report.

The hypothesis is that several functional questions recur across risk levels, not that every domain requires identical evidence kinds. A low-stakes internal recommendation may use a lighter base module than a benefits decision, clinical summary, procurement action, or external communication. Domain and forum modules may add evidence kinds, applicability rules, or consequences. Comparability across modules has to be established before two results are said to meet the same standard \citep{messick1995validityStandards}.

\subsection{From Action-Level Verdicts to Program-Level Claims}

Action-level verdict vectors don't project themselves. A program claim has to declare its source result, projected outcome, bearer, unit, target population, conditions and transformations, time and version range, and tolerance. It also needs an intended use and minimum useful reach, a warrant plan, and a prospective rule for failure, independent retest, demotion, or retirement. The selection problems below bear on that warrant; they don't supply the missing declaration.

Applying the test across a program introduces three distinct selection problems. First, if a program evaluates several configurations, prompt sets, or runs with noisy value estimates and selects the estimated maximum, the chosen estimate is optimistically biased on average even when each estimate is conditionally unbiased. This is the optimizer's curse \citep{smith2006optimizersCurse}. Its remedies require held-out evaluation or an explicit prior and error model; Smith and Winkler's \enquote{disciplined skepticism} is Bayesian updating of the noisy estimates, not a generic penalty for any curated record.

Second, a proponent may purposively report persuasive cases or omit unfavourable ones. That is evidence-selection or reporting bias, not automatically the formal optimizer's curse. The record should disclose the selection mechanism, candidate set, inclusion rule, and relevant deployment or target denominator; any skeptical adjustment beyond held-out evidence is an audit proposal that needs its own assumptions.

An incident-derived evaluation set is one purposive selection mechanism. It can test whether a reconstructed failure recurs and whether a regression control responds under the reconstructed conditions, but it doesn't estimate prevalence or performance across a deployment. The record should therefore preserve the incident trigger, candidate pool, inclusion rule, reconstruction fidelity, stochastic repeat protocol, and results rather than treating a selected replay as representative \citep{openai2026longHorizonSafety}.

Third, a sample-selection process determines which affected cases become visible at all. Learning, psychological, and compliance costs are plausible filters on who can challenge a decision, but don't identify a deployment's selection mechanism by themselves \citep{moynihanHerdHarvey2015administrativeBurden}. Likewise, a selected sample doesn't generally identify population quantities without additional graphical assumptions or external data \citep{bareinboimTianPearl2014selectionBias}. Complaint counts are warning signals, not prevalence estimates. An assertion of \enquote{no complaints} is evidence of low incidence only under a supported observation model for exposure, channel access, delivery, capacity, opportunity to complain, recording, and routing. Without the relevant quantities and assumptions, absence or scarcity of observed complaints doesn't estimate the incidence of defective actions. A Pearlean selection diagram becomes appropriate only when the target is a specified causal or statistical quantity and the source--target differences and recovery assumptions are stated. It can't carry a changed legal rule, action meaning, forum, or evidential standard across domains.

Cancellation is a separate aggregate defeater. In controlled question answering, task-content-irrelevant context produces item-level improvements and degradations that can largely cancel in mean accuracy while leaving substantial harmful-tail degradation \citep{zhang2026illusionRobustness}. A program-level assurance claim should retain item or action transitions and risk-relevant tails wherever repeated paired conditions permit them. Random sampling from a declared frame addresses favourable case selection from that frame; it doesn't cure noncoverage, missingness, selective reporting, or an average in which oppositely signed failures erase one another.
