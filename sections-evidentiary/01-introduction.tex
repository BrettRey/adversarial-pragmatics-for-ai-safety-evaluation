\section{Introduction}

AI risk-management guidance already treats governance, transparency, accountability, documentation, and monitoring as core trustworthiness practices \parencite{nist2023artificial}. NIST's generative-AI profile adds a more specific emphasis on governance, content provenance, pre-deployment testing, and incident disclosure \parencite{nist2024generative}. These requirements produce evidence. They don't yet settle the action-level sufficiency question: given a contested AI action, what body of evidence would support a finding that the action stayed within the authority under which the system was permitted to act?

The question is narrower than explainability, broader than logging, and more forensic than ordinary model documentation. Explainability asks why an output or prediction took the shape it did. Logging asks what events were recorded. Model documentation asks what a system is intended to do and how it was evaluated. Evidentiary assurance asks whether the record is strong enough to support a later authority claim about a particular action.

The target action may be a recommendation, decision, communication, tool call, transaction, record update, or content-generation act with legal or organizational effect. The central evidentiary burden is to connect that act to a valid source of authority, a scoped delegation instrument, the relevant system state, the procedure actually followed, and records whose authenticity and integrity can survive later review.

Recent work on digital money makes the infrastructural point in another domain: a safe liability can still become fragile when it runs on a congested settlement rail \parencite{klee2026fragility}. AI evidence has a comparable rail. A model may be capable, a permission may be valid, and a policy may be well specified, but the later authority claim still depends on the infrastructure that carries action from instruction to tool use, logging, approval, preservation, and review.

Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-rail} summarizes that rail. Each step contributes a different kind of evidence; none replaces the others. The final review claim depends on the chain linking authority, configuration, execution, oversight, preservation, and contestability.

Administrative-law record review gives the closest legal analogue. A reviewer needs more than a plausible result. The record has to support review of authority, procedure, reasons, evidentiary basis, and reviewability. AI action records add a further burden: they have to distinguish the system that produced the act from the human or institutional actor that remains responsible for authorizing and relying on it.

The paper belongs beside, but not inside, the delegation-assurance paper. Delegation assurance gives AI-security and evaluation communities an evidence standard for justified machine action. This paper develops the legal, governance, provenance, and digital-evidence side of that standard. Its contribution is an evidence-bundle account: a way to specify what has to be preserved before an organization can later defend the claim that an AI action remained authorized, scoped, attributable, procedurally valid, and contestable.
