\section{Introduction}

AI risk-management guidance already treats governance, transparency, accountability, documentation, and monitoring as core trustworthiness practices \parencite{nist2023artificial}. NIST's generative-AI profile adds a more specific emphasis on governance, content provenance, pre-deployment testing, and incident disclosure \parencite{nist2024generative}. These requirements produce evidence. They don't yet settle the action-level review question: given a contested AI action, what record would make the authorization claim evaluable?

The question is narrower than explainability, broader than logging, and more forensic than ordinary model documentation. Explainability usually concerns the relation between a model and its output: why a prediction or generated answer took the shape it did. Logging identifies recorded events, and model documentation states intended use and evaluation context. Evidentiary assurance concerns the relation between a deployed system and an authorized action: whether records of authority, configuration, context, tool use, oversight, and preservation support a later authorization claim.

Whole-process reviewability already spans commissioning, development, operation, and investigation, with records shaped by the review context \parencite{cobbeLeeSingh2021reviewable}. The narrower question here concerns the noncompensatory evidence bundle for one contested action-level authorization claim.

The target action may be a recommendation, decision, communication, tool call, transaction, record update, or content-generation act with legal or organizational effect. The central evidentiary burden is to connect that act to the claimed source of authority and expose its validity, scope, required factual and legal predicates, relevant system state, and procedure actually followed, using records whose authenticity and integrity can survive later review.

Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-rail} summarizes six converging evidence rails for delegated AI action. The rails have no temporal order and aren't substitutable ingredients. Each supports a distinct family of subclaims, and a strong showing on one doesn't compensate for a missing showing on another.

Administrative-law record review gives a useful generic legal analogue. A reviewer needs more than a plausible result. The record has to support review of authority, procedure, reasons, evidentiary basis, and reviewability. The analogy is schematic and jurisdiction-sensitive: Canada, the United States, the European Union, and Australia don't share a single doctrine of administrative record review. AI action records add a further burden: they have to distinguish the system that produced the act from the people and organizations responsible for authorizing and relying on it.

Delegation assurance \parencite{reynolds2026delegationAssurance} supplies the typed authorization claim and transition semantics. Evidentiary assurance asks whether the preserved record makes that claim evaluable and what evidentiary verdict it warrants, rather than redefining standing or valid transition. Its contribution is an evidence-bundle account: a way to specify what has to be preserved before an organization can later defend, revise, or abandon the claim that an AI action remained authorized, scoped, attributable, procedurally valid, and contestable.
