\section{Introduction}

AI risk-management guidance already treats governance, transparency, accountability, documentation, and monitoring as core trustworthiness practices \parencite{nist2023artificial}. NIST's generative-AI profile adds a more specific emphasis on governance, content provenance, pre-deployment testing, and incident disclosure \parencite{nist2024generative}. These requirements produce evidence. They don't yet settle the action-level sufficiency question: given a contested AI action, what body of evidence would support a finding that the action stayed within the authority under which the system was permitted to act?

The question is narrower than explainability, broader than logging, and more forensic than ordinary model documentation. Explainability usually concerns the relation between a model and its output: why a prediction or generated answer took the shape it did. Logging identifies recorded events, and model documentation states intended use and evaluation context. Evidentiary assurance concerns the relation between a deployed system and an authorized action: whether records of authority, configuration, context, tool use, oversight, and preservation support a later authority claim.

The target action may be a recommendation, decision, communication, tool call, transaction, record update, or content-generation act with legal or organizational effect. The central evidentiary burden is to connect that act to a valid source of authority, a scoped delegation instrument, the relevant system state, the procedure actually followed, and records whose authenticity and integrity can survive later review.

Figure~\ref{fig:evidence-rail} summarizes the evidence chain for delegated AI action. Each step contributes a different kind of evidence; none replaces the others. The final review claim depends on the chain linking authority, configuration, execution, oversight, preservation, and contestability.

Administrative-law record review gives a useful generic legal analogue. A reviewer needs more than a plausible result. The record has to support review of authority, procedure, reasons, evidentiary basis, and reviewability. The analogy should remain jurisdiction-sensitive: Canada, the United States, the European Union, and Australia don't share a single doctrine of administrative record review. AI action records also add a further burden: they have to distinguish the system that produced the act from the human or institutional actor that remains responsible for authorizing and relying on it.

Delegation assurance \parencite{reynolds2026delegationAssurance} defines the action-level security claim: whether a machine action remained within delegated authority. Evidentiary assurance develops the evidentiary side of that claim: what records, controls, and review practices would support it. Its contribution is an evidence-bundle account: a way to specify what has to be preserved before an organization can later defend the claim that an AI action remained authorized, scoped, attributable, procedurally valid, and contestable.
