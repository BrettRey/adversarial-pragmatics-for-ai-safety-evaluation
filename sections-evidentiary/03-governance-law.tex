\section{Governance and Legal Sources}

The intake target for this section is cross-jurisdictional, with emphasis on Canada, the European Union, the United States, and the United Kingdom or Australia where official sources are especially probative. The relevant sources fall into three groups.

First, AI-governance instruments require logs, documentation, intended-purpose limits, instructions for use, human oversight, impact assessment, monitoring, recourse, and disclosure. These instruments generate evidence but usually stop short of specifying what evidence is sufficient to prove that a concrete action stayed within delegated authority.

Second, electronic-evidence and electronic-signature law already has mature concepts for authenticity, integrity, attribution, secure signatures, and tamper detection. Those doctrines aren't AI-specific, but they provide strong analogues for AI action traces.

Third, public failures and adjudicated disputes show the practical stakes. Automated public-benefits schemes, algorithmic sentencing tools, and institutional chatbots all raise variants of the same question: what record would let an affected person or reviewer determine whether the system acted within the authority the institution could validly delegate?
