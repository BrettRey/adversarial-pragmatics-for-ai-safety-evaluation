\section{Evidence Primitives}

Table~\ref{tab:evidentiary-primitives} separates ten review questions that often get collapsed. They aren't ten coequal authorization propositions. Authority source, delegability, scope, and any regime-conditioned predicates or procedures bear primarily on \(J_A\). Attribution and record quality bear primarily on \(J_R\). Answerability and contestability bear on \(J_B\) and \(J_C\). The versioned crosswalk in \path{assurance/evidentiary/crosswalk.csv} maps these primitives, a legacy six-part evidence grouping, the canonical nodes, and the operational record layers onto one graph. Its meta-assessment and projective rows are deliberately left unmapped to the older review primitives rather than being forced into a false equivalence.

\term{Decision basis} is provisional as a separate primitive. Its distinctive question is whether the rule actually used was linked to the rule's required predicates through defensible data definitions and transformations. The record should also map the rule used back to its claimed source of authority. A perfectly attributable execution can otherwise preserve an unsupported inference without exposing it as such. Decision-basis evidence enters \(J_A\) only when \(R\) makes the relevant predicate a condition of authority. Otherwise it bears on merits, legality, or \(J_R\). It should be folded into neighbouring nodes if matched-case testing shows that it detects no additional failure.

\term{Assessment validity} is different from authority validity. It concerns the evidence and rationale supporting a proposed interpretation or use of results from the configured review procedure, not whether a source could confer authority. The procedure, reviewer population, record population, regime, forum, evidential standard, burden allocation, and version are therefore part of the assessment object \citep{messick1995validityAssessment}.

Action-level review requires two linked records rather than one generic owner field. The answerability primitive uses an \term{answerability-bearer matrix} to identify each candidate individual or organization, the linked role or office, duty source, effective interval, succession route, bearer-specific powers, and present reachability. The contestability primitive uses an \term{object-level review/remedy forum matrix} to identify a complaint, appeal, regulatory, judicial, or internal-remedy forum, its institutional or jurisdictional basis, record access, competence, applicable independence, duties, and powers to review, correct, halt, sanction, compensate, or remediate where those powers apply. The forum that applies the configured evidentiary-assurance procedure, \(F_{\mathrm{review}}\), is a parameter of the assessment; an object-level remedy forum is evidence assessed under \(J_C\). The bearer doesn't acquire either forum's powers, and none of these records establishes legal liability on its own.

\term{Record access} isn't binary. The forum matrix should record who possesses the material; whether the reviewer receives it voluntarily, by regulator request, or through compulsion; what confidentiality protections govern that access; and whether any part is publicly available. The adverse-signal record should identify the route by which a signal became reviewable (routine reporting, a regulator request, a complaint, or civil discovery) without treating review access as authority to disclose it publicly.

Procedure contains two records that ordinary logs often omit. An \term{adverse-signal record} binds warnings, legal advice, adverse review decisions, complaints, and incidents to their time, applicability, recipients, relevant duties, deadlines, dispositions, escalations, and downstream changes. An \term{expected-governance-control record} binds a triggering condition to the act required, responsible role, due time, completion or disposition, and monitoring coverage. Here \term{governance control} means a prescribed organizational or technical check. Its existence doesn't establish effective maintenance or corrective causal control. Failure to record completion counts against a review claim only when the prospective applicability map requires the control and the expected completeness of its logging is supported.

When an incident generates a new evaluation, training intervention, monitor, or redeployment condition, those records also need lineage: the triggering incident class, selection and inclusion rule, reconstruction method and known fidelity limits, evaluation version, repeated-run protocol, observed results, review decision, and deployment change. OpenAI reports such a sequence of incident-derived evaluation, longer-rollout training, trajectory monitoring, small-set replay, and limited redeployment; it notes that replay rollouts weren't guaranteed to pursue the same action because of randomness and imperfect environment reconstruction \citep{openai2026longHorizonSafety}. This is a useful record pattern, not evidence that the selected cases estimate incident prevalence or that the safeguards caused deployment-wide safety.

Explanation, provenance, logging, and documentation supply parts of the linked evidence required by the action-level record-and-verdict test.
