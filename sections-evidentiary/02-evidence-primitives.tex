\section{Evidence Primitives}

The framework separates seven evidentiary primitives.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Authority.} Who or what authorized the system to act?
  \item \textbf{Scope.} What acts, targets, tools, data classes, thresholds, and exceptions were delegated?
  \item \textbf{Attribution.} Which system instance, organizational actor, model version, tool, and configuration produced or approved the action?
  \item \textbf{Procedure.} Which required steps, reviews, approvals, notices, or escalation paths were followed?
  \item \textbf{Authenticity.} Are the records what they purport to be?
  \item \textbf{Integrity.} Are the records intact, complete, and protected against undetected alteration?
  \item \textbf{Contestability.} Can an affected person, auditor, regulator, court, or internal reviewer inspect enough of the record to challenge the action?
\end{itemize}

These primitives overlap with explainability, provenance, audit logging, and documentation, but none of those neighbouring concepts is sufficient on its own. Explanation helps with reasons. Provenance helps with lineage. Logging helps with reconstruction. Delegated-authority assurance needs the bundle.
