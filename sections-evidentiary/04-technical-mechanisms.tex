\section{Technical Mechanisms}

Technical mechanisms supply pieces of the evidentiary bundle. Structured execution logs record what happened and when. Provenance graphs connect entities, activities, agents, and transformations. Content provenance systems bind claims, manifests, hashes, signatures, and timestamps to digital artefacts. Append-only transparency logs make alteration and equivocation more detectable. Bills of materials expose components and dependencies. Runtime policy enforcement can check explicit constraints before action. For tool-using language-model systems, adversarial language can enter through retrieved content and lower-priority instructions, so the evidentiary record also has to preserve instruction source and priority \parencite{greshake2023not,wallace2024instruction,debenedetti2024agentdojo}.

Each mechanism has a limit. A log can be incomplete. Provenance is descriptive rather than normative. A signature can preserve a false assertion. A bill of materials can show components without proving authority. A formal policy can miss the natural-language or organizational exception that mattered in the event. The evidentiary-assurance task is to combine these mechanisms into a sufficiency standard for action-level review.
