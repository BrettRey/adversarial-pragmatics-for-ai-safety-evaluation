\section{Research Program}

The intake shows a real gap, but not a vacuum. Explainability helps with reasons. Model cards, datasheets, and factsheets help with intended use and lifecycle disclosure. Provenance standards help with lineage. Content-authenticity systems and secure logs help with integrity. AI-governance instruments help with documentation, oversight, and recourse. Electronic-evidence law helps with authenticity and best-evidence concepts. Public-sector automation failures show why reviewability matters.

The next research task is to derive a minimum evidence bundle for delegated AI action. A candidate bundle includes the delegation instrument, policy and permission state, configuration snapshot, model and tool versions, input context, retrievals, tool calls, approvals, overrides, safety interventions, timestamps, human-oversight records, and integrity protections.

The central claim should remain narrow. The literature doesn't ignore evidence. It disperses evidence across neighbouring fields. The contribution is to integrate those fields around one question: what proves that a specific AI action remained within delegated authority?
