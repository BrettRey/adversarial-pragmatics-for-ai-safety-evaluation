\section{Research Program}

The research task is to turn neighbouring tools for reasons, lineage, integrity, governance records, digital evidence, and reviewability into an action-level sufficiency test. Table~\ref{tab:minimum-evidence-bundle} gives one candidate minimum bundle for delegated AI action: delegation instrument, policy and permission state, configuration snapshot, model and tool versions, input context, retrievals, tool calls, approvals, overrides, safety interventions, timestamps, human-oversight records, and integrity protections.

This bundle is a research hypothesis, not a finished standard. It needs domain-specific calibration. A consumer chatbot, a medical summarizer, a benefits triage tool, and a procurement assistant don't require the same evidence depth. But the domains differ by burden and risk, not by primitive. Each has to answer the same review questions: who authorized action, what was delegated, what happened, what record supports the claim, who remains answerable, and how the action can be challenged or corrected.

The financial-reporting analogy suggests how such a test could mature. A delegation-assurance standard could define control objectives for authorization, scope, information flow, persistence, tool use, record integrity, and remediation, then test whether a deployment's controls make action-level reconstruction possible.

The central claim remains narrow. The literature doesn't ignore evidence; it disperses evidence across neighbouring fields. The contribution is to integrate those fields around one question: what supports the claim that a specific AI action remained within delegated authority?
