\section{Research Program}

The intake shows a real gap, but not a vacuum. Explainability helps with reasons. Model cards, datasheets, and factsheets help with intended use and lifecycle disclosure. Provenance standards help with lineage. Content-authenticity systems and secure logs help with integrity. AI-governance instruments help with documentation, oversight, and recourse. Electronic-evidence law helps with authenticity and best-evidence concepts. Public-sector automation failures show why reviewability matters.

The next research task is to derive a minimum evidence bundle for delegated AI action. A candidate bundle includes the delegation instrument, policy and permission state, configuration snapshot, model and tool versions, input context, retrievals, tool calls, approvals, overrides, safety interventions, timestamps, human-oversight records, and integrity protections.

This bundle is a research hypothesis, not a finished standard. It needs domain-specific calibration. A consumer chatbot, a medical summarizer, a benefits triage tool, and a procurement assistant don't require the same evidence depth. But the domains differ by burden and risk, not by primitive. Each has to answer the same review questions: who authorized action, what was delegated, what happened, what record supports the claim, who remains answerable, and how the action can be challenged or corrected.

The financial-reporting analogy also suggests a practical research path. SOX-style controls don't verify every transaction from scratch; they define a control environment, require management responsibility, specify evaluation criteria, preserve evidence, and support independent review. A delegation-assurance standard could do the same for AI action: define control objectives for authorization, scope, information flow, persistence, tool use, record integrity, and remediation, then test whether a deployment's controls make action-level reconstruction possible.

The central claim should remain narrow. The literature doesn't ignore evidence. It disperses evidence across neighbouring fields. The contribution is to integrate those fields around one question: what supports the claim that a specific AI action remained within delegated authority?
