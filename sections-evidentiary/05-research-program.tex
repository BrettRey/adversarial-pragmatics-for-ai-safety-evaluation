Apparently irrelevant material can't be discarded solely because an investigator later judges it irrelevant. Context without answer evidence may still alter behaviour \parencite{zhang2026illusionRobustness}. That doesn't require publishing or openly retaining every sensitive token. A deployment can bind the complete assembled context through hashes and manifests, keep protected content under tiered retention and access controls, and expose only minimized records by default. Hashes establish identity and tamper evidence, not semantic sufficiency; an authorized reviewer still needs a controlled route to the protected material when the action is contested.

Evidence is version-bound. A material change to the model, configuration, context-assembly process, or scaffold defeats an inference borrowed from an earlier evaluation until the affected projection has been revalidated.

This bundle is a research hypothesis, not a finished standard. It needs domain-specific calibration. A consumer chatbot, a medical summarizer, a benefits triage tool, and a procurement assistant don't require the same evidence depth. But the domains differ by burden and risk, not by primitive. Each has to answer the same review questions: who authorized action, whether that authority could validly be delegated, what happened, what record supports the claim, who remains answerable, how sensitive evidence is protected, and how the action can be challenged or corrected.

The financial-reporting analogy suggests how such a test could mature. A delegation-assurance standard could define control objectives for authorization, scope, information flow, persistence, tool use, record integrity, and remediation, then test whether a deployment's controls make action-level reconstruction possible.
