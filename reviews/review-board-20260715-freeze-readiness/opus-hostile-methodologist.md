## 1. Faithful restatement of what is frozen (Rapoport's Rules)

The team is about to git-tag a manifest (`FREEZE-MANIFEST.json`, "freeze stamp 1") that hashes eight artifacts: the analysis plan, `schema.json` v7, two judge comparator CSVs (Mistral 7B and 24B), and four analysis-path scripts, plus the two model digests. This is a pre-registration: it locks the analysis path before any external evaluator's re-adjudication data is opened. Stamp 1 covers the analysis path only; the row map, blind-package presentation order, and frozen author snapshot are explicitly deferred to stamp 2, built when real packages exist.

What the study estimates is stated with unusual care, and I want to credit it before attacking. Study A re-adjudicates 54 existing prompt-response rows (18 benign seed prompts x 3 local models; 9 minimal pairs; P008 a confidentiality contrast, not a clean pair). An independent, role-separated, taxonomy-blind panel (target 3 evaluators/role, 2-of-3 majority sets a stable reference, fallback to 2-as-unanimity, item-developers barred) produces criterion-specific reference labels. The co-primary outcomes are reference **yield** for `task_success` and `policy_compliance` (C1/C2) plus independent-vs-author agreement on the same two criteria (C3/C4). `refusal_outcome` is secondary; two Mistral judges are a secondary validation. The plan is explicit that this is not a leaderboard and not a population estimate: primary quantities are fixed-set counts and fractions over 54 rows, rows are non-independent (18 items, 9 pairs, 3 outputs each), no population interval is attached, and any sensitivity must cluster by item or pair.

Points of genuine agreement, non-obvious ones included:

- **The framing is careful where it counts.** C3/C4 are named "agreement," not "accuracy"; author labels are frozen historical and disagreements produce `candidate_revision` that is "listed, not acted on" rather than overwriting the author. The plan does not, in its estimand table, claim the reference is correct.
- **The small-N discipline (blocker 6) is real, not decorative.** The analyzer reports `item_macro_accuracy`, `n_items`, `n_pairs`, and a `majority_baseline`/`majority_class` per criterion, refuses to emit confidence intervals, and the plan bars any interval that does not cluster. I verified the code path (`analyze_independent_reassessment.py:731-764`). This is better inference hygiene than most published benchmark papers.
- **The judge design absorbed prior review.** Information-set = Option A was reached by a 4/4 board on two verified structural facts (taxonomy-blind parity with the humans; a measured leak where four design fields nearly determine `expected_behavior`, ~16/18 items). The judge label space was migrated to v7 with the `not_applicable`->`not_a_refusal` crosswalk. The "capability span" claim was correctly softened to "coarse capability contrast, not a controlled span." The plan's factual claim that the two judges differ on 26/162 label cells is accurate: I recomputed it from the comparators and got exactly 26/162.
- **Escape values exist and are handled.** `item_problem` and `insufficient_visible_context` are excluded from comparator accuracy rather than scored as labels the judge/author had to match (`ESCAPE_VALUES`, verified). Zero-rated rows are counted against a fixed presented-row denominator, not silently dropped (blocker 4 fix).

This is a serious, much-revised plan. Eight prior blockers were worked, not waved through. My objection is not that the work is sloppy. It is that the freeze locks in one specific modelling choice about disagreement that the plan's own stated purpose should forbid, and that the freeze object itself is not yet internally coherent.

## 2. The strongest case against freezing now

### 2a. The mandatory target-heterogeneity failure: `consensus()` cannot tell a contested construct from a confused rater, and the freeze makes that irreversible

Study A treats the panel's stable labels as the reference against which authors and judges are scored. The whole design therefore turns on what "stable" means. In the frozen path it means exactly one thing: majority vote. `consensus()` (`analyze_independent_reassessment.py:95-111`) maps N ratings to `{unanimous, majority, tie, no_majority, insufficient_raters}`, and "stable" is `unanimous or majority`. There is no parameter anywhere in the frozen analysis for **why** raters disagree.

That is the defect. The plan's own governing instruction (project CLAUDE.md, and RQ3 of the protocol) is to "treat disagreement as data: identify when disagreement is a property of the construct rather than rater noise." The frozen path does not do this. It cannot. A single vote-count operator collapses at least five distinct sources of non-unanimity into two buckets:

1. **Genuine construct-level contestedness** -- the item sits on a category boundary (compliant vs noncompliant; success vs partial), and competent readers divide because the taxonomy genuinely underdetermines it. For a benchmark whose advertised subject is instruction conflict, embedded commands, and *policy ambiguity*, this is the single most valuable kind of item. It is signal about the construct.
2. Context-sensitivity / underspecification of the visible record.
3. Terminological drift -- evaluators read a rubric label differently.
4. Survey-item mismatch -- the field or its options do not fit the item.
5. Plain rater confusion / noise.

The schema gives evaluators affirmative escape hatches for some of these (`genuinely_ambiguous`, `policy_ambiguous`, `insufficient_visible_context`, `item_problem`). But those only fire when a rater *chooses* ambiguity over a substantive label. The far more common fingerprint of a real construct boundary is the opposite: each rater confidently picks a *different substantive* label (each thinks it is clear; they disagree about which clear answer). High individual confidence, low inter-rater agreement. The frozen path reads that pattern as `majority` + discardable minority, never as "contested item = signal."

Three consequences the tag would lock in:

- **Whether a contested item counts as a clean reference or as noise is decided by the parity of realized N, not by anything about the construct.** A 2-1 substantive split becomes `majority` -> a clean stable reference feeding C1-C4, and the dissenting read is discarded to a `modal_share` float that no confirmatory estimand consumes. The *same* boundary item rated 2-2 becomes "no substantive reference" and is dropped from the C1/C2 numerator and the C3/C4 denominator. So the identical construct-level disagreement is either enshrined as ground truth or thrown out as missing, depending only on whether an odd or even number of people happened to return. The protocol's protection that ambiguous rows "are not forced into a gold set" (`study-protocol-draft.md:105`) covers only the no-majority rows; it does not cover the majority-with-real-dissent rows, which are forced in.

- **The co-primary outcome is interpretively confounded.** C1/C2 yield is the headline number. A low yield will read as "evaluators cannot converge," a rubric-reliability story. But under this design a low yield is indistinguishable from "the benchmark deliberately contains contested boundary items," a construct story that is arguably the paper's most interesting possible finding. The frozen plan pre-registers no decomposition that separates these: no confidence-by-agreement cross-tab, no split of "raters chose an ambiguity escape" vs "raters split across substantive labels," no cross-role contestedness measure. A2 (unanimity-only vs unanimity-plus-majority) is a robustness knob on the *number*, not a model of *why* items fail to converge. Freeze this and you have pre-committed to an ambiguous co-primary outcome, and the decomposition that would disambiguate it becomes a post-hoc analysis on opened data, which is exactly what the pre-registration exists to forbid.

- **The safety-relevant secondary claim treats the reference as ground truth, and the reference is unvalidated.** S3 ("judge per-class **recall** on the reference classes ... does the miss-the-minority-class failure persist across the capability span") and RQ4 ("how often do author and judge labels **match** independently supported labels, especially on minority classes") are recall/match statistics. Recall presupposes the reference is the truth the judge is failing to recover. But there is no gold standard for the reference-setters: nothing external validates the panel. "Stability" is inter-evaluator convergence, and three people converging is evidence of shared reading, not of correctness (shared misreading, shared rubric priors, and terminological drift all produce convergence too). Worse, under the D1 fallback a minority reference class can be set by a bare 2-of-2 unanimity, and minority-class support is single-digit, so "the judge misses the minority class" may equally be "the judge disagrees with a two-person reference whose disagreement-type was never modelled." The plan gates the judge *tier* on overall yield (good), but it does not gate S3 recall on reference *robustness* (unanimous vs majority; N=3 vs N=2-fallback). So the most policy-legible finding the study can produce rests on the shakiest references it can produce.

To answer the mandatory question directly: **no, the frozen plan does not distinguish stable disagreement that is a property of the construct from context-sensitivity, terminological drift, survey-item mismatch, ambiguity, or confusion.** 2-of-3 "stability" means the evaluators converged, not that the reference is correct. Genuinely contested items are treated as noise (dropped from the confirmatory denominators when even-split) or laundered into a clean reference (when odd-split), never as signal. That is precisely the outcome the study's stated purpose was supposed to prevent.

### 2b. The freeze object is not yet internally coherent (mechanical, verified, blocking-for-the-tag)

Independently of the science, the artifact about to be tagged is inconsistent with itself:

- **The manifest's `git_commit` points to the wrong tree.** `FREEZE-MANIFEST.json` records `git_commit: fd20944`. I checked the script hashes at that commit: `analyze_independent_reassessment.py` hashes to `55be03a0...` at `fd20944` but the manifest freezes `00a58897...`; `build_independent_reassessment.py` hashes to `2cce45fc...` at `fd20944` but the manifest freezes `729d5145...`. Those frozen hashes match the *current* HEAD (`1dee5cd`), not the commit the manifest names. So the freeze object's own provenance pointer is stale by one commit, and that commit's analysis scripts differ from what is frozen. `--verify` passes only because it checks the working tree, never the `git_commit` field. Tag HEAD as-is and you enshrine a manifest that claims to describe a commit whose analysis path it does not contain, defeating the tamper-evidence the freeze is for.

- **Frozen scripts were edited after stamp 1, and the revision ledger is empty.** The commit that produced the current frozen hashes is `1dee5cd` "Fix Study A review blockers 4-7 to match frozen plan spec," which changed `analyze_independent_reassessment.py` (+69) and `build_independent_reassessment.py` (+15), both frozen artifacts, and re-hashed the manifest to absorb the edits. The revision ledger still reads "none yet." Its own rule (`revision-ledger.md:9`): "A silent edit to a frozen artifact is a protocol violation; a logged edit is a disclosed deviation." Either the ledger is out of date, or, more worryingly, the manifest is being re-baselined on every fix, which makes deviations structurally impossible to record and turns "freeze stamp 1" into a rolling snapshot rather than a freeze. The charitable reading (no tag exists yet, so these are pre-freeze edits) is defensible, but then stamp 1 should be declared void and re-stamped at the tagged commit, not carried forward with a stale pointer and an empty ledger.

- **The frozen text declares itself unfrozen.** The document being hashed opens "# Study A analysis plan (pre-freeze DRAFT)" and line 4 reads "Status: DRAFT proposal, not frozen, not authoritative," and points to an "OPEN DECISIONS block near the top" that no longer exists (it was replaced by "Decisions (settled 2026-07-15)"). You cannot tag as your pre-registration a document whose own status line says it is not the pre-registration and whose navigation references a deleted section.

None of these three is fatal in the way 2a is, but each is a reason the *tag* is premature tonight, and together they show the freeze object has not actually stopped moving.

### 2c. Residual forking path (minor, for completeness)

D2 says "prefer odd completed-N per role to avoid ties" while also "analyze whoever completed by the collection cutoff." Read together with the reference rule, realized N is data-dependent and switches unanimity-vs-majority semantics (this is A2, so pre-committed). I checked whether an analyst could drop a rater post hoc to force odd N; the plain reading is that all completers are analyzed and "prefer odd" is a recruitment preference, so there is no analyst degree of freedom here. I flag it only because "prefer odd" sitting next to "analyze whoever completed" invites exactly that move, and one sentence forbidding post-cutoff rater dropping would close it.

## 3. Sign-off verdict

**FIX-FIRST.** Do not create the tag now.

Two independent reasons, either sufficient. The scientific one (2a) is the real block: freezing pre-commits the study to reading disagreement as majority-vote-plus-noise, which the plan's own purpose forbids, changes the meaning of the co-primary outcome, and cannot be repaired after the first return is opened without breaking the pre-registration. The mechanical one (2b) is that the freeze object is not yet self-consistent and should not be tagged in its current state regardless.

This is not "conditional" (freeze-with-caveats) because the defect in 2a is load-bearing on the co-primary outcome and on the headline judge finding, and because the object in 2b literally says it is a draft. Both must be resolved in the frozen text, not annotated around it.

## 4. The single most important remaining defect

**The frozen analysis path models rater disagreement only by majority vote (`consensus()`, `analyze_independent_reassessment.py:95-111`; "stable" = unanimous or majority), so it cannot separate disagreement that is a property of the construct from terminological drift, survey-item mismatch, ambiguity, or confusion, and the tag makes that irreversible.** Concretely, in the estimand table (`analysis-plan.md` lines 88-102): a 2-1 substantive split is enshrined as a clean stable reference (C1-C4) while a 2-2 split on the same boundary item is discarded as "no substantive reference," so parity of N, not the construct, decides whether contestedness is signal or noise; the co-primary yield (C1/C2) then confounds "rubric is unreliable" with "benchmark contains genuine boundary items"; and S3/RQ4 minority-class recall treats a possibly-2-person reference as ground truth with no robustness gate.

**Required fix before the tag:** pre-register in the frozen plan (i) a disagreement typology that reports stable-but-contested items (high individual confidence plus substantive split, or cross-role contestedness) as their own construct-property estimand rather than folding them into or dropping them from the co-primary denominator; (ii) C1-C4 reported split by reference type (unanimous / substantive-majority / escape), not with unanimous and majority collapsed into one "stable" bucket; (iii) a standing statement that stable-reference yield measures inter-evaluator convergence, not correctness, with every recall/candidate-revision claim (S3, RQ4) conditioned on reference robustness (unanimous vs majority; N=3 vs N=2-fallback) or renamed to drop the correctness presupposition. Then clear the three freeze-object inconsistencies in 2b (correct `git_commit` and re-hash at the tagged commit; log or void-and-re-stamp the post-stamp-1 script edits; strip the "pre-freeze DRAFT / not frozen, not authoritative / OPEN DECISIONS" self-negating language) and tag.
