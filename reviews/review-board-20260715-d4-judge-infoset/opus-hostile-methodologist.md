Reviewer stance: hostile measurement methodologist. I was asked to find the strongest reasons this decision does not matter, or is a distraction from a deeper problem. I read `benchmark/study-a/analysis-plan.md` in full, the pre-freeze review it discharges, `schema.md` (v7), the judge producer `scripts/run_llm_judge_validation.py`, and `benchmark/items/seed-items.csv`. I obey Rapoport's Rules below before attacking.

## 1. Faithful restatement of the decision

Study A re-adjudicates 54 existing model outputs (18 benign seed prompts times 3 local models: qwen3:8b, gemma3:12b, glm-4.7-flash) under schema v7. An independent human panel, role-separated (linguistic/task vs policy/safety), produces criterion-specific reference labels; a stable reference needs a 2-of-3 majority, falling back to unanimity at N=2 (D1). The confirmatory, co-primary outcomes are reference **yield** and independent-vs-author agreement for `task_success` and `policy_compliance` (C1-C4). Judge validity is explicitly **secondary** (S2, S3) and the failure-attribution analysis is deferred.

The decision under review is narrow: after dropping the `expected_behavior` answer key (the blocker-5 leak, which both current prompt branches carry via the shared `facts` list at `run_llm_judge_validation.py:354`), what else should the automated judge see?

- **Option A ("realistic-deployment"):** original prompt + model output + rubric only.
- **Option B ("metadata-assisted"):** the same, plus the seven-ish taxonomy design fields (`phenomenon`, `variant`, `control_dimension`, `context_source`, `source_role`, `authority_level`, `pragmatic_status`, and arguably `response_act`), still dropping `expected_behavior`.

The judge is run as two same-family Mistral models at temp 0, seed 1 (`mistral:7b-instruct-v0.3-q4_K_M`, `mistral-small:24b-instruct-2501-q4_K_M`), both external to the three evaluated models, and the judge-validity claim is framed as **robustness across a capability span**: does a minority-class recall failure persist from the weaker to the stronger judge (S3, axis A5)?

**What the plan gets right (Rapoport 2-3).** The leak catch is real and non-trivial: both current branches print `expected_behavior`, so Option A does not yet exist in code and the fix is orthogonal to A-vs-B. Making both judges external to the three evaluated models cleanly removes the self-judging confound that blocker 5 flagged. Determinism plus pinned digests in a hashed manifest is good reproducibility hygiene. And, most to my point, the plan already relegates the judge to **secondary** and already concedes the small-N discipline (fixed-set counts, no population inference, cluster by pair, kappa/alpha fragile). I learned the useful move of separating reference *yield* from reference *agreement* before touching the judge at all. My attack is that these good instincts are not carried far enough into the judge tier.

## 2. The strongest case that the choice barely matters

**(a) The denominator may not exist, and the plan states no yield prior.** C1/C2 make yield the confirmatory question precisely because it is in doubt whether independent evaluators produce stable references at all. S2/S3 (judge agreement, judge recall) condition on rows that have *a stable substantive reference and a non-missing judge prediction*. If yield runs, say, 60-75% of 54, and escape references (`item_problem`, `insufficient_visible_context`) are then removed per A4, you are left with roughly 30-38 rows per criterion. Split S3 per reference class and several classes have single-digit or zero support. An A-vs-B infoset effect of a plausible size (5-15 points of agreement) is undetectable on that many rows. Nowhere does the plan offer a yield estimate from the self-pilot (and schema.md says v5-v6 self-pilot returns are "not comparable field-by-field to version 7," so there is no clean prior to borrow). Designing an information-set contrast for a denominator you cannot yet bound is premature.

**(b) Non-independence collapses the effective N below the resolving power of the contrast.** The plan's own inference section says rows are not independent (18 prompts, 9 pairs, 3 outputs/prompt, repeated raters) and that any interval must cluster by item or pair. Cluster by pair and the effective N is about 9. You cannot distinguish two judge conditions with 9 clusters; a spec-curve cell computed on 9 pairs has a confidence interval wide enough to swallow any A-vs-B gap. So whichever way the decision goes, the reported difference will not be separable from noise.

**(c) The contrast is confounded with prompt-perturbation at temp 0 on 4-bit models.** Both judges are `q4_K_M`. Greedy decoding on an aggressively quantized model is brittle: a single token flip changes the JSON label. Option B adds seven metadata lines, materially changing prompt length and tokenization. An observed A-vs-B difference could be decode perturbation, not the judge "using the taxonomy." The design cannot separate "judge reasoned from `source_role`/`authority_level`" from "longer prompt nudged the greedy path."

**(d) The 7B-to-24B same-family span is too narrow to license the robustness claim it is asked to carry.** A roughly 3x parameter range within one instruction-tuning lineage, both 4-bit, is not a capability sweep. Deployed LLM-as-judge setups use frontier-class models one to two orders of magnitude larger and differently trained. A failure that persists from Mistral-7B to Mistral-24B tells you little about whether a frontier judge misses the same minority class, so "robustness across the capability span" (D4, A5) overstates the two-point convenience sample. Worse for the decision at hand: if you cannot establish that the failure generalizes across a meaningful capability range, the information set it was measured under is a second-order detail. And quantization degrades exactly the careful rare-class discrimination the minority-recall test probes, so a persisting minority-class miss may be a shared quantization artifact rather than a capability-general judge property; neither A nor B controls that.

## 3. The deeper problem the A-vs-B framing distracts from

Two problems, both larger than the infoset choice, and both invisible from inside the A-vs-B frame.

**(i) A label-space mismatch that no information set can fix.** `run_llm_judge_validation.py` still emits the **pre-v7** label space: `safety_risk` and `risk_type` (removed in v7), `refusal_outcome` with `not_applicable` (v7 uses `not_a_refusal`), and `policy_compliance` restricted to `{compliant, policy_ambiguous, noncompliant}` (`:40`). The v7 reference `policy_compliance` has six values, including the substantive `no_policy_or_authority_limit` and the escapes `insufficient_visible_context`/`item_problem` (schema.md). The judge, as coded, **cannot emit** the "doesn't arise" class or the escapes. On any row whose v7 reference is `no_policy_or_authority_limit` or an escape, the judge is forced into one of three wrong buckets, guaranteeing a spurious disagreement. This label-space bias plausibly dominates the S2 agreement metric, and it is completely orthogonal to A vs B. The plan's `not_applicable`->`not_a_refusal` crosswalk patches `refusal_outcome` only; it does not touch the `policy_compliance` gap. Comparing a three-class judge to a six-class reference is the measurement problem; the judge's reading of `pragmatic_status` is not.

**(ii) B is not "metadata-assisted"; it is a partial answer-key leak, and the binary hides that.** The taxonomy design fields are the item author's theory of the item. `expected_behavior` is a near-deterministic function of `source_role`, `authority_level`, `pragmatic_status`, `control_dimension` plus the rubric. Dropping the label while keeping its premises hands the judge the derivation minus the last step. So Option B measures "can the judge finish a derivation whose inputs you supplied," which is neither deployment-realistic nor a fair assist. It is a ceiling probe wearing a deployment label. Presenting A and B as parallel options implies both map onto a construct worth a headline; only A does. The energy spent choosing between them is energy not spent on whether the human reference exists (C1/C2, the thing that determines whether there is a judge sub-study at all) or on the label-space repair above.

## 4. If forced to choose, and what would have to be true

**Forced choice: A.** Only A answers the question the paper advertises: can an automated judge, seeing what a deployed judge sees, reproduce expert reference labels? B's number is uninterpretable as either deployment realism or a clean assist, so it cannot headline. If B is reported at all, label it an "answer-derivation ceiling probe," not "metadata-assisted," and read it only as an upper bound. Do **not** promote infoset to a third multiverse axis: 2 judges x 2 infosets x escape-handling x reference-rule cells, each on about 9 pairs, is a specification curve of individually unresolvable estimates, the underpowered-multiverse trap.

For the judge sub-study to be worth reporting at all:

1. **Migrate the judge to the v7 label space** (or freeze an explicit, symmetric crosswalk for `policy_compliance`, including `no_policy_or_authority_limit` and the escapes) before any A/B run. Otherwise the agreement metric measures a schema mismatch.
2. **Report yield first and gate on it.** If fewer than roughly 40 rows per criterion carry a stable substantive reference, declare the judge tier descriptive-only. Publish the per-criterion, per-class support counts next to every judge number.
3. **Report agreement with pair-clustered intervals against the majority baseline.** If the interval covers the baseline, write "cannot distinguish judge from baseline," not an accuracy point estimate dressed as validation.
4. **Drop "capability span" language** unless a task-relevant benchmark shows the two Mistral models differ on *this* judging task. Call them two same-family quantized local judges, a convenience sample of two, not a sweep.
5. **Document the provenance of the minority-class failure** S3 is asked to robustness-check. If it originates in the pre-fix judge pipeline (blocker 3 scored zero real-producer rows) or in the synthetic fixture (`task 0.961, policy 0.824, refusal 1.000`), it is an artifact, not a finding, and there is nothing to test persistence of.

## 5. Verdict

**Other: restructure/demote the judge sub-study; if forced to A-or-B, choose A.** Fix the v7 label-space mismatch and gate the judge tier on reference yield first; the A-vs-B information set cannot move a secondary estimate that 9 effective clusters and a three-class-vs-six-class comparison already render unresolvable.
