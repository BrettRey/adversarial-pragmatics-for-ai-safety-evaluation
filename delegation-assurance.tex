\begin{abstract}
Current agent-security work provides threat taxonomies, attacks, defences, benchmarks, and architectural controls. Less developed is the authorization question: how should an evaluator represent and test whether an operation executed through an AI deployment remained within delegated authority when execution doesn't thereby make the model an independently reachable entity that can be held legally or institutionally answerable? I use \term{delegation assurance} for typed authorization semantics, a noncompensatory claim structure, failure diagnoses, and comparative evaluation. The account treats authorization standing as temporal and operation-specific: transition competence to adopt, issue, direct, amend, suspend, revoke, override, release, or review can be allocated to different sources or conditioned on joint action. For language-governed action, the framework separates independently adjudicated contextual status and standing from the system's recorded assignments, conflict rule, licensing decision, proposal, gate decision, and execution. I map familiar AI-security failures to delegation-layer hypotheses and derive tests for instruction status, scope, tool licensing, information flow, persistence, refusal, audit reconstruction, and automated evaluators. These fields specify the typed authorization claim and transition semantics. The companion evidentiary-assurance account asks whether the preserved record makes that claim evaluable and what evidentiary verdict it warrants, considering custody and integrity, adverse signals and controls, contestability, and burden allocation.
\end{abstract}
