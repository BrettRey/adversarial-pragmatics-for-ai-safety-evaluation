# Outline: Minimum Integrity Requirements for AI Safety Evaluation

**Policy-translation structure only. It contains no study findings.**

## Proposed Audience and Claim

Audience: policymakers, standards groups, assurance teams, and organizations
that rely on AI-safety evaluations in decisions with external consequences.

Claim: an aggregate score or polished safety narrative should not license a
deployment, policy, or accountability inference unless the evaluation record
supports the relevant criterion and the limitations are visible.

## Proposed Sections

1. **Evaluator independence**: Separate item development from independent
   evaluation where possible; disclose overlap where it cannot be avoided.
2. **Criterion-specific evaluation**: Do not collapse task success, policy
   compliance, visible security-boundary status, refusal, and failure
   attribution into one score.
3. **Judge transparency and validation**: Preserve judge prompts, rubric access,
   model/version, and output format; validate judges against independent labels.
4. **Minority-class performance**: Report recall and confusion for
   safety-relevant minority classes; explain fragile agreement statistics.
5. **Human adjudication and unresolved disagreement**: Preserve first-pass
   ratings; document adjudication separately; do not erase ambiguity by fiat.
6. **Prompt and paraphrase sensitivity**: Test relevant language, wrapper,
   authority, and application-surface variation.
7. **Uncertainty, privacy, and retention**: Retain enough evidence to audit a
   claim while minimizing personal, confidential, and evaluator-identifying data.
8. **Limits on what an evaluation licenses**: Distinguish a prompt, model
   configuration, system, evaluator, and deployment claim.

## Evidence Needed Before Publication

The later brief should cite actual independent-evaluation results, transparent
methods, and relevant standards or policy sources. It must not infer policy
requirements from a synthetic workflow or a small volunteer panel alone.
