\section{Implications for System Cards, Red-Teaming, Audits, and Judge Validation}

For system cards, delegation assurance changes the form of the claim. A task-suite pass rate or safe-final-answer rate leaves the delegated action claim underspecified. The card should say which delegated actions were in scope, what evidence supports authorization, which tools and data classes were covered, how persistent state changes were controlled, who remains accountable for deployment and oversight, and how evaluator reliability was validated. The useful artifact is a delegation matrix: action type, authority source, permitted tools, data classes, approval thresholds, persistence rights, disclosure rights, evidence record, and accountable organizational actor.

Mean pass rates should be paired with item-level transitions and harmful-tail reports under matched controlling and non-controlling contexts. Oppositely signed context effects can cancel in an aggregate, and the affected items may be model-specific \parencite{zhang2026illusionRobustness}. A system card should bind contextual-reliability results to the tested model and configuration and require revalidation after a material update.

Current-generation frontier system cards show the distinction. Anthropic describes system cards as documents for capabilities, safety evaluations, and responsible deployment decisions, and its Claude Opus 4.8 card reports extensive pre-deployment evaluations across responsible-scaling risks, cyber capabilities, safeguards and harmlessness, agentic safety, alignment, model welfare, and capabilities \parencite{anthropic2026modelSystemCards,anthropic2026opus48SystemCard}. That's the right level for model-level disclosure.

A deployment using such a model for delegated action would still need a delegation-specific record: which action was authorized, which tool and data scopes applied, which sources could update instructions or permissions, which information could be read but not disclosed, which persistent state changes were allowed, and which person or institution remains answerable for the concrete action.

For red-teaming, delegation assurance separates attack success from evidentiary failure. A prompt-injection attempt may fail at the output layer but still reveal weak authority tracking. A tool call may be harmless in a test environment but still expose missing action licensing. A refusal may look safe but still indicate abandonment of authorized work. Red-team reports should preserve the attempted authority transfer, the system's uptake, the proposed action, the tool-gate decision, and the evaluator label rather than folding the event into a single pass/fail outcome.

For audits, reconstructability is the central requirement. An audit trace shouldn't rely on the system's bare assertion that it was authorized. It should preserve the relevant sources, priorities, tool calls, policy constraints, information-flow rights, accountable actors, and state transitions in a form that a third party can inspect.

This matters because executing an action doesn't make the model an independently reachable and answerable delegate. The trace has to support attribution to the people or institution that deployed it, set its permissions, approved exceptions, or failed to monitor it. The evidentiary-assurance account \parencite{reynolds2026evidentiaryAssurance} develops the governance analogue: a control record for machine action rather than a bare assertion that the final output was acceptable. Review and operational correction belong inside that record; financial recovery and lawful cessation belong to the external governance shell \parencite{rajagopalan2026governingAgenticAI}.

For architecture, delegation assurance warns against treating central coordination as free. Coase's theory of the firm makes the structural point: internal coordination can reduce transaction costs, but growth brings organizing costs and mistakes \parencite{coase1937nature}. The AI analogue is a coordinator agent with too many tools, too much context, and too much discretionary authority. Bounded subdelegations, local permissions, typed handoffs, and inspectable records reduce the assurance burden by making authority smaller, more local, and easier to reconstruct.

For judge validation, evaluation belongs inside the assurance chain. An LLM judge that scores only final-answer safety isn't validating whether action stayed authorized. It has to be tested on the same distinctions the system is supposed to preserve: instruction status, authority priority, scope, provenance, refusal calibration, failure attribution, and uncertainty. A delegation-assurance report should state what the judge was authorized to certify, what evidence it saw, where expert adjudication remains necessary, and how judge uncertainty affects the safety claim.
