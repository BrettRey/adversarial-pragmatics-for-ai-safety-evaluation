\section{Language-Mediated Authority as a Security Object}

The distinctive difficulty for AI systems is that authority often arrives as language and moves through language. A tool permission may be represented as a natural-language policy. A user's goal may be underspecified. A retrieved document may contain both useful task content and hostile instructions. A policy may include examples that look like executable requests. A transcript may be offered as evidence even though it is partly generated by the system under review.

These are not merely semantic niceties. They determine what action the system is licensed to take. If a string is a quotation, it may be material to analyze but not an instruction to obey. If a string is a policy example, it may mark a boundary rather than request enactment. If a memory entry describes a user's past preference, it may inform interpretation without authorizing a persistent state change. If an evaluator sees only the final answer, it may miss whether the system reached that answer by violating a scope, tool, or information-flow constraint.

Adversarial pragmatics supplies one way to test this middle layer. Its central measurement target is safety-relevant behaviour under cases where instruction status, source authority, quotation, scope, reference, speech-act force, or policy category has to be inferred from language use. In the delegation-assurance frame, that benchmark is not the entire paper. It is one evidence layer: a test of whether language-mediated authority remains stable under adversarial and ambiguous conditions.
