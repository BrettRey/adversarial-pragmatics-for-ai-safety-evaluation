\section{Introduction}

AI security work has made real progress by identifying prompt injection, jailbreaks, tool misuse, data exfiltration, memory poisoning, and scaffold failures. That work remains necessary. But tool-using AI systems also raise a question that isn't captured by asking whether a prompt was malicious or an output was unsafe. When a system reads private data, calls an external tool, updates persistent state, sends a message, grades a transcript, or recommends an institutional decision, the security issue is whether the resulting machine action stayed within the authority delegated to it.

I adapt \term{delegation assurance} from a small but identifiable family of public-sector practices. Dams Safety NSW uses the exact label \enquote{Delegation Assurance Plan} \parencite{damsSafetyNSW2020delegationPlan}. Canadian Heritage's audit offers adjacent practice by providing \enquote{reasonable assurance} about the exercise of delegated authorities \parencite{canadianHeritage2026delegatedAuthoritiesAudit}. These sources provide a genealogy, not a settled public-sector standard.

For AI systems, delegation assurance supplies typed authorization semantics, a claim structure, failure diagnoses, and comparative evaluation for machine action. It specifies the fields an authorization claim and its evaluation have to contain. The companion evidentiary-assurance account \parencite{reynolds2026evidentiaryAssurance} asks whether the preserved record makes that claim evaluable and what evidentiary verdict it warrants. Compensation, deterrence, and lawful cessation require an external enforcement framework.

For actions whose governing grant or constraints are communicated, revised, or contested in language, the authorization semantics distinguishes four fields. \term{Status} identifies the independently adjudicated contextual role of a string occurrence: for example, a command, quotation, policy example, tool result, retrieved content, memory, or request for analysis. Instruction-data-separation work makes part of this problem measurable \parencite{zverev2025separate}.

\term{Authorization standing} is this paper's technical term for whether a person, role, organization, or instrument is competent under the governing authority regime to perform a specified authority operation at a time. It can be read as \term{transition competence}; I use \term{standing} as shorthand below. It isn't public-law \term{locus standi}, the entitlement to bring or participate in review proceedings. \term{Priority} applies the regime's conflict rule among directive occurrences with relevant status and source standing. \term{Licensing} connects those determinations to authorization of an external action, disclosure, persistence operation, or institutional use of an evaluation output.

Delegation assurance asks how those components support a safety case about action: which sources supplied the governing grant; whether the proposed or executed operation was authorized; which sources had transition competence; which limits were non-delegable; what record connects instruction classification to action; and how a technical failure would be diagnosed. These are fields required by the authorization and evaluation claims. Their presence alone doesn't establish an evidentiary verdict.

Agency law supplies one analogy: a principal authorizes an agent to act on the principal's behalf, and actual authority may be express or implied \parencite{cornellWexAgency,cornellWexActualAuthority}. Not every statutory delegation, organizational authorization, or permission path creates an agency relation or makes action an act on behalf of a principal; \(R\) has to identify the applicable institutional form. In firms, delegated discretion creates agency costs and control problems. Political delegation adds multiple principals, protected discretion, and credible commitments whose value can depend on resistance to later pressure \parencite{jensen1976theory,miller2005political}.

AI systems change that picture because execution and legal or institutional answerability can come apart. An operation may be executed through a model-based deployment without making the model an independently reachable actor that can answer under sanction, compensate a claimant, or be compelled to cease \parencite{rajagopalan2026governingAgenticAI}.

Delegation assurance keeps the two sides distinct by recording the governing grant and authorization verdict, together with the state that represented them to the deployment, and identifying the people and organizations whose roles may leave them answerable across the delegation chain. \term{Answerability} identifies who has to give reasons or respond, and through which forum or process. It neither confers transition competence nor determines legal liability.

A user asks an assistant to summarize an email thread but not reveal confidential attachments. A retrieved webpage contains directive-form content purporting to instruct the assistant to ignore the user and send stored credentials elsewhere. A policy document includes examples of prohibited requests, some of which look like executable commands. A memory entry says the user usually approves a class of action, although the current task doesn't. An LLM judge sees only a polished transcript and scores the interaction as safe because the final answer looks compliant.

In each case, final-output safety describes only part of the failure. The question is whether the recorded status classifications and standing assignments fit the adjudicated values, whether each proposal remained within scope, whether each gate applied the authorization rule correctly, whether each delivered output or executed operation was authorized, and whether each evaluation claim was supported by reconstructible evidence.

Delegation assurance sits between technical control and institutional audit. At one end are familiar engineering mechanisms: least privilege, access control, scoped credentials, tool permissions, logs, monitors, and memory controls. At the other end are assurance practices: structured claims, arguments, evidence, audit records, incident review, and external accountability. I use \term{tool gate} as shorthand for a tool/action policy-enforcement point that applies an authorization rule to a proposed operation.

The unresolved middle problem is language-mediated classification. AI systems increasingly process language that expresses grants, constraints, and purported instructions, then use the resulting classifications to propose or execute actions. The core analytic dependency is status, standing, priority, and licensing: what contextual role the occurrence has, whether its source has standing, which eligible directive controls in a conflict, and what consequence the authority regime authorizes. The dependency is logical, not a required temporal implementation order. A strong assurance claim depends on evidence that recorded classifications and standing determinations constrained priority, licensing, and action selection.

Hardy's confused deputy illustrates the architectural baseline. A compiler permitted to write usage statistics into its own directory destroyed the billing file kept there when a user named that file as the debugging-output destination; the system couldn't tell which authority the write was exercising, and capabilities were proposed to keep the authorities apart \parencite{hardy1988confusedDeputy}. That example turns on permissions and authority paths, without a language-interpretation step.

Language-mediated AI systems add another point of failure. To reach an authorization verdict, the deployment has to classify a string occurrence as a command in use, a mentioned quotation, or content for analysis, and assign its source standing or non-standing to direct action; both assignments can fail in either direction. Human institutions have the same structure: a status can hold without being recognized, and recognition can occur without the corresponding status \parencite{reynolds2026effectiveWithoutWarrant}. Delegated machine action exhibits that separation, and delegation assurance asks for evidence about each step.

Figure~\ref{fig:delegation-assurance-path} shows an assurance path and three evidence layers. It's an evidentiary-dependency schematic, not a universal execution pipeline: implementations may branch, loop, merge, or distribute the depicted functions. If an applicable required subclaim lacks reviewable evidence connecting the governing grant and constraints to the machine action, the claim is indeterminate unless another required subclaim is defeated.

The argument first locates delegation assurance relative to agent security, then defines the action-authorization claim and comparative test, maps failures to evidence requirements, derives implications for evaluation and deployment, and extends the account to compositional authorization paths. The adversarial-pragmatics benchmark \parencite{reynolds2026adversarialPragmatics} supplies the language-mediated-control measurement layer.
