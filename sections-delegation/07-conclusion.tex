\section{Conclusion}

Delegation assurance supplies typed authorization semantics and a noncompensatory claim structure for action authorization, failure diagnosis, and comparative evaluation. These tools matter because AI deployments combine language processing, tools, persistent memory, and evaluation in ways that can obscure the distinctions among governing grants, recorded authorization states, proposed operations, executed operations, and evaluator findings.

The framework offers instruction-status classification, temporal and operation-specific authorization standing, higher-order transition validity, protected mandates, action licensing, information flow, persistence, audit reconstruction, and automated-evaluator validation as diagnostic dimensions for familiar AI-security failures. These fields specify the typed authorization claim and transition semantics. Evidentiary assurance asks whether the preserved record makes that claim evaluable and what evidentiary verdict it warrants.

Delegation assurance stakes its usefulness on the comparative test of Section~\ref{sec:comparative-test}: the typed fields should improve held-out, target-matched reviewer reconstruction or behaviour prediction beyond baselines using capabilities, per-call authorization, causal attribution, and generic context controls. The framework has no scalar measure of authority, and required subclaims don't compensate for one another. Its scope remains limited to cases where a deployment's action permissions, limits, authority transitions, or authorization evaluation are at issue; it doesn't turn every model error into a delegation failure.
