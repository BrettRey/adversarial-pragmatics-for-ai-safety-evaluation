## 9. Re-evaluation after the expert-evaluator paper

I read the complete manuscript in
`papers/retarget/expert-grammaticality-judges-evaluators/`, its source-
verification record, decisions, status, and rejection diagnosis. My earlier
inference—“their ratings answer the research questions, therefore they are
participants”—was too categorical. It repeats the literal-response inference
that the manuscript explicitly argues against.

### Best substantive classification

Study A is designed as **expert evaluation of non-human prompt--response
objects**, not as a study of the evaluators. The strongest evidence is the
alignment between the design and the manuscript's role test:

| Study A feature | Role-test reading |
|---|---|
| Fixed 54-row object set; no population inference to raters | The outputs and rubric are the targets, not a population of people |
| Explicit role-specific codebook and fixed label vocabularies | Prescriptive expert evaluation under a statable, challengeable criterion |
| Purposive qualification and item-developer exclusion | Evaluators selected as measurement personnel, not sampled respondents |
| Agreement used to decide whether an object gets a supported panel label | Instrument/procedure calibration, not a conclusion about evaluator traits |
| Author--panel and judge--panel comparisons | Comparisons among object-labelling procedures |
| Stable evaluator IDs and person-disjoint roles | Repeated-rating linkage, independence, and criterion separation |

The [official TCPS scope interpretation on citizen science](https://ethics.gc.ca/eng/policy-politique_interpretations_scope-portee.html)
also recognizes that people may assist research by gathering data about
non-human objects without thereby being participants. That is not a binding
ruling on Study A, but it is a closer official analogy than the ordinary-course
staff example alone.

### Mixed-role pressure points to remove

The evaluator classification is credible only if the project does not turn the
people into an outcome. Four changes sharpen that boundary:

1. Reframe RQ1/RQ2 from “how often evaluators produce labels” to: **For what
   fraction of the fixed 54 prompt--response objects does the prespecified,
   role-specific evaluation procedure yield a supported substantive label?**
2. Remove evaluator burden/timing from exploratory research outcomes and remove
   measured timing as a hard collection gate. Use Brett's conservative
   **30--40 minutes per block** only as an administrative workload estimate.
3. Constrain confidence and rationales to item-level measurement/adjudication
   metadata. Do not estimate rater severity, drift, subgroup effects, confidence
   calibration, reasoning styles, individual performance, or any population
   quantity over evaluators.
4. Describe contested ratings and cross-role differences only as properties of
   object--criterion cells or the rubric. Do not interpret them as differences
   between linguistic people and policy people, because role and person pool are
   confounded.

Panel network mix remains an independence/conflict-of-interest control, not a
sampling claim. Stable IDs remain useful, but “pseudonymous” should not carry
the argument: privacy is not what makes someone an evaluator.

### Institutional status remains separate

The manuscript itself calls Canada's literal definition its hardest case,
treats its focus-of-inquiry reading as an argument, and says local boards decide
scope. The design therefore supports a strong **evaluator** classification to
present to Humber; it does not permit either Codex or the repository to issue a
binding institutional exemption.

Humber's public wording is not fully explicit about an off-duty, unfunded,
independent employee project with no Humber resources or sanction. The narrow
and proportionate step is a written scope determination, not an automatic full
REB application and not a self-declared exemption. No recruitment, package
distribution, external return, or open-collection tag should precede that
determination.

### Operational simplification still follows

- Do not make a dedicated mailbox a scientific requirement. Use whatever
  access-controlled return channel the final scope/data plan permits. MFA simply
  means a password plus a second authentication factor.
- Remove the synthetic email-transfer test and its hash-bound transfer-test
  evidence as a hard gate. Receipt-time schema, evaluator-ID, role, package,
  block, and duplicate checks protect return integrity. This does not remove
  the separate requirement to retain hash-bound institutional-scope evidence.
- Keep person-disjoint roles, but treat the private roster check as the real-
  world control; a cryptographic attestation only proves file stability.
- Keep a plain-language evaluator agreement covering voluntariness, workload,
  attribution/confidentiality, withdrawal, retention, and recognition. Convert
  it to participant consent only if Humber classifies the design that way.
- Treat the accepted August dates as superseded. Humber's
  [current deadline page](https://humber.ca/ppdr/services/research/humbers-research-ethics-board/reb-deadlines.html)
  says its REB does not operate from June 19 through September 1, 2026.
